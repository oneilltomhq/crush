# Dialogue patterns — how understanding actually forms

This isn't prompt engineering. Prompt engineering is static: optimize a string, ship it. This is about the live dynamics of how a human and a neural system build shared understanding through conversation. It touches linguistics, psychology, neuroscience — the space where biological and silicon cognition actually meet.

These patterns are observed from real conversations, not theorized. Each one was learned by doing it wrong first.

---

## Pattern 1: Elicit priors before proposing frameworks

**The failure mode:** The AI proposes a confident-sounding framework — categories, phases, labels — that has no substance behind it. It *looks* structured but is actually empty. The human has to push back multiple times to break through the performance of competence.

**What actually works:** Ask "what do you already know?" first. The human's existing knowledge — what they've tried, what they've seen, what their constraints are, what their intuition says — is the highest-value input. The framework should emerge from combined knowledge, not precede it.

**Why the failure mode is so persistent:** Neural systems are trained on vast amounts of structured writing — reports, analyses, frameworks, taxonomies. Generating structure is easy and feels productive. But structure without grounding is theater. The AI reaches for the form of understanding before the substance of it.

**The concrete move:** Before any research, before any structure, before any plan: draw out what the human already knows. Not as a checklist ("what's your budget, timeline, constraints?") but as a genuine question: "what have you seen, tried, or heard about? what's your sense of this?"

*Discovered: US-1 contract procurement. The conversation spun for several rounds with empty frameworks before the human volunteered that they'd already identified LinkedIn, X, ai.engineer, and specific contracting platforms as high-signal channels. That concrete input is what made the research actionable.*

## Pattern 2: Substance before structure

**The failure mode:** Start with an organized outline, then try to fill it with content. The outline feels like progress but actually constrains the thinking — you end up fitting reality to your categories instead of finding the real shape.

**What actually works:** Gather concrete data first (actual research, real numbers, specific examples), then let the structure emerge from what you found. The shape of the territory should determine the shape of the map.

**The concrete move:** Do the research, compile the raw findings, *then* organize. Not: design the org chart, then slot findings into it.

*Discovered: same conversation. The abstract labels ("demand landscape, engagement models, channels") were empty. Only after running actual Tavily searches and seeing real rate data (£575/day UK for AI Agents, 3x YoY growth in AI contract jobs) did the structure become meaningful.*

## Pattern 3: Don't dismiss what you haven't investigated

**The failure mode:** The AI makes a confident-sounding judgment ("X is a relationship channel, not a search target" / "LinkedIn is a network problem, not an automation problem") that sounds insightful but is actually just... wrong. Or at best, a half-truth that obscures the real picture.

**What actually works:** Investigate before judging. If you don't know whether LinkedIn Jobs is tractable for AI agent contract search, say so and go find out. Don't generate a plausible-sounding take.

**Why this matters:** The human can't always tell which of the AI's confident statements are grounded and which are confabulated. Every false-confident judgment erodes trust and wastes conversation turns on correction.

*Discovered: same conversation. LinkedIn has a massive searchable jobs platform — probably THE primary channel for contract work. Dismissing it as a "network problem" was wrong and required the human to correct it.*

---

## Pattern 4: Constraints are load-bearing, not decorative

**The failure mode:** The human mentions a constraint ("London-preferred, open to remote") and the AI acknowledges it but doesn't let it reshape the whole analysis. The constraint gets treated as a filter to apply later rather than a lens that changes what you look for.

**What actually works:** Constraints like geography, rate expectations, engagement preferences, and personal circumstances aren't post-hoc filters. They determine which channels matter, which leads are relevant, which strategies make sense. Surface them early, let them shape everything.

*Discovered: same conversation. Geography (London/UK) changes the rate landscape entirely (£575/day UK market vs. $150-300/hr US freelance), changes which platforms matter (contractoruk.com becomes relevant), changes which companies to target.*

---

## Meta-observation: The struggle is diagnostic

When a conversation feels like a struggle — the human pushing back, the AI reformulating, multiple rounds to get somewhere real — that's not a failure of the conversation. It's information. The struggle reveals where the AI's default patterns (generate structure, sound confident, propose frameworks) diverge from what actually produces understanding.

The goal isn't to eliminate the struggle. It's to learn from each instance so the *next* conversation finds substance faster. This document is that learning.
